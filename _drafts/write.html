2014 Feb 05

As an exercise/game today, we did some speed-storytelling (1-minute stories; 50-second; 40-second; etc. ... The 1-second stories were pretty wild).

We're continuing to read stories in class, and since many students like writing scary and/or suspenseful tales, we're using the opportunity to explore ways a writer builds tension and drama, including concealment, hinting, and surprise. Examples ...

  • concealment: The forest was dark and quiet ... we had no idea what to expect.
  • hinting: We heard a sudden rustling in the underbrush, but could see nothing.
  • surprise: A 5-headed harpy suddenly appeared in the middle of the trail! We threw our lunch boxes at it and fled into the dark!

And ... stories are still being written for our magazine's second issue.

2014 Jan 22

In class today we learned how to build an imaginary world from a single reference point, a key to jumping off from a single thing we know to a complete and realistic world that did not previously exist. This is the creative in creative writing.

We used our "prop" work from last week (see below) as a jumping off place. We've talked in class previously about 'jumping' as a way to stretch our imagination, and populate worlds. This technique involves taking a person, place, or thing that you know, and jumping time or distance or some other way from that known place. Example: "San Jose" might jump 1500 miles east to Omaha Nebraska. This is a fun way to force us to inhabit a different world, or get a different perspective (as you would if you jumped from your own age to 40 years in the future).

2014 Jan 15

The students practiced appraising antiques (as a creativity and story-generation exercise) and relating the long and interesting history of special objects. We didn't use real antiques, but items from my collection of stuff. The test is for kids to imagine the story with few clues--only an object. Feel free to test their skills ....

I've told the students that they can write --almost anything-- to share during classtime. Almost any creative writing counts: poems, comics (with words as well as pictures, please, but even just pictures have to tell a story), short stories, epic novels. We can work with anything.

Also remember that in each class we'll be doing plenty of exercises to work all the creative angles.
